# The Seven Wonders of the World

## The New Seven Wonders (2007)

In 2007, following a worldwide poll of over 100 million votes organized by the Swiss-based New Seven Wonders Foundation, seven modern wonders were officially announced. The campaign was led by Canadian-Swiss filmmaker Bernard Weber.

### The Great Wall of China

The Great Wall of China stretches over 21,196 kilometres (13,171 miles) across northern China. Built over many centuries beginning as early as the 7th century BC, the wall reached its most complete form during the Ming Dynasty (1368–1644). The wall was constructed to protect Chinese states from nomadic invasions from the north, particularly from the Xiongnu. Made from stone, brick, tamped earth, wood, and other materials, the Great Wall is the world's longest man-made structure. It runs from Dandong in the east to Lop Lake in the west, passing through mountains, deserts, and grasslands.

### Petra, Jordan

Petra, also known as the "Rose City" due to the colour of the sandstone from which it is carved, is an ancient city located in southern Jordan. Founded by the Nabataeans, an Arab people, around the 4th century BC, Petra served as the capital of the Nabataean Kingdom. The city is renowned for its rock-cut architecture and water conduit system. Its most iconic structure, Al-Khazneh (The Treasury), stands 40 metres (130 ft) tall and was carved directly into the sandstone cliffs. Petra was largely unknown to the Western world until Swiss explorer Johann Ludwig Burckhardt rediscovered it in 1812.

### Christ the Redeemer, Brazil

Christ the Redeemer (Portuguese: Cristo Redentor) is a colossal Art Deco statue of Jesus Christ in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil. Standing 30 metres (98 ft) tall atop an 8-metre (26 ft) pedestal on the summit of Corcovado Mountain, the statue has a wingspan of 28 metres (92 ft). It was constructed between 1922 and 1931 and was designed by French sculptor Paul Landowski in collaboration with Brazilian engineer Heitor da Silva Costa. The statue is made of reinforced concrete and soapstone and weighs approximately 635 metric tons.

### Machu Picchu, Peru

Machu Picchu is a 15th-century Inca citadel located in the Eastern Cordillera of southern Peru on a mountain ridge 2,430 metres (7,970 ft) above sea level. It is situated above the Sacred Valley, 80 kilometres (50 miles) northwest of Cuzco. Built around 1450 CE during the reign of Inca emperor Pachacuti, Machu Picchu was abandoned roughly 100 years later following the Spanish conquest. The site was largely unknown to the outside world until American historian Hiram Bingham brought it to international attention in 1911. The estate contains more than 150 buildings, including temples, sanctuaries, parks, and residences.

### Chichen Itza, Mexico

Chichen Itza was a large pre-Columbian city built by the Maya civilization. Located in the Yucatán Peninsula of Mexico, the site flourished between roughly 600 and 1200 CE. The most prominent structure is El Castillo (The Castle), also known as the Temple of Kukulcan, a step pyramid approximately 30 metres (98 ft) high with 91 steps on each of its four sides, plus one step at the top platform — totalling 365 steps, equal to the number of days in the Mayan solar year. During the spring and autumn equinoxes, the setting sun creates a shadow that resembles a serpent descending the northern staircase of El Castillo.

### The Roman Colosseum, Italy

The Colosseum, also known as the Flavian Amphitheatre, is an oval amphitheatre in the centre of Rome, Italy. Built of travertine limestone, tuff, and brick-faced concrete, it is the largest ancient amphitheatre ever built. Construction began under Emperor Vespasian in 70 CE and was completed in 80 CE under his successor Titus. The Colosseum could hold between 50,000 and 80,000 spectators, who watched gladiatorial contests, animal hunts, executions, re-enactments of famous battles, and dramas based on classical mythology. It is estimated that over 400,000 people and over one million animals died in the Colosseum over the centuries of its operation.

### The Taj Mahal, India

The Taj Mahal is an ivory-white marble mausoleum on the south bank of the river Yamuna in Agra, India. It was commissioned in 1632 by Mughal emperor Shah Jahan to house the tomb of his favourite wife, Mumtaz Mahal, who died during childbirth. The tomb is the centrepiece of a 17-hectare (42-acre) complex that includes a mosque and a guest house. Construction of the mausoleum was completed in 1643, but work continued on other phases until 1653. More than 20,000 artisans and workers participated in the construction. The Taj Mahal stands 73 metres (240 ft) tall and features a large white dome surrounded by four minarets.

## The Seven Wonders of the Ancient World

The original Seven Wonders of the Ancient World were catalogued by ancient Greek writers, with the classic list assembled around the 2nd century BC. Of the seven, only one still stands today.

### The Great Pyramid of Giza

The Great Pyramid of Giza, built around 2560 BC for Pharaoh Khufu (also known as Cheops), is the only ancient wonder still largely intact. It stands 138.5 metres (454 ft) tall today, though it originally reached 146.5 metres (481 ft). For over 3,800 years it was the tallest man-made structure in the world. The pyramid contains approximately 2.3 million stone blocks, each weighing between 2.5 and 15 tonnes.

### The Hanging Gardens of Babylon

The Hanging Gardens of Babylon are described as a remarkable feat of engineering: a series of tiered gardens containing trees, shrubs, and vines, said to have been built in Babylon, near present-day Hillah in Iraq. They were reportedly commissioned by the Neo-Babylonian king Nebuchadnezzar II around 600 BC as a gift for his homesick wife Amytis, who missed the lush green hills of her homeland. Uniquely among the ancient wonders, no definitive archaeological evidence of the gardens has been found, leading some historians to question whether they actually existed.

### The Statue of Zeus at Olympia

The Statue of Zeus at Olympia was a giant seated figure approximately 13 metres (43 ft) tall, made by the Greek sculptor Pheidias around 435 BC at the sanctuary of Olympia in Greece. Constructed of ivory plates and gold panels over a wooden framework, it depicted the god Zeus on a throne. It was considered the most famous sculpture in the ancient Greek world. The statue was eventually moved to Constantinople, where it was destroyed in a fire in the 5th century CE.

### The Temple of Artemis at Ephesus

The Temple of Artemis was a Greek temple dedicated to the goddess Artemis, located in Ephesus near present-day Selçuk in Turkey. The final version stood 137 metres (450 ft) long and 69 metres (225 ft) wide. It was burned down on 21 July 356 BC by Herostratus, a man seeking fame, on the same night Alexander the Great was born. The temple was subsequently rebuilt but was destroyed again by the Goths in 268 CE.

### The Mausoleum at Halicarnassus

The Mausoleum at Halicarnassus was a tomb built between 353 and 350 BC for Mausolus, a satrap of the Persian Empire, and his sister-wife Artemisia II of Caria, in present-day Bodrum, Turkey. The structure stood approximately 45 metres (148 ft) in height and was decorated with sculptural reliefs on all four sides. The English word "mausoleum" is derived directly from the name Mausolus. The tomb was destroyed by successive earthquakes between the 11th and 15th centuries CE.

### The Colossus of Rhodes

The Colossus of Rhodes was a statue of the Greek sun-god Helios, erected in the city of Rhodes on the Greek island of the same name by the sculptor Chares of Lindos in 280 BC. Standing approximately 33 metres (108 ft) high, it was one of the tallest statues of the ancient world. The statue was destroyed by an earthquake in 226 BC, just 54 years after its construction. After its collapse, the broken remains lay on the ground for over 800 years.

### The Lighthouse of Alexandria

The Lighthouse of Alexandria was built by the Ptolemaic Kingdom between 280 and 247 BC on the island of Pharos in Alexandria, Egypt. With a height estimated between 100 and 130 metres (330–430 ft), it was one of the tallest man-made structures for many centuries and guided sailors into the busy harbour. It was damaged by three earthquakes between 956 and 1323 CE and gradually fell into ruin. The Arabic word for lighthouse, "manara", is believed to derive from the Pharos lighthouse.
